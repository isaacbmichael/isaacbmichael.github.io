PRAMS Data Weighting — National Delivery and Automation

Oct 2024 – Mar 2025

I co-led a nationwide effort to turn raw files from many jurisdictions into clear, consistent reports over a demanding six-month season. I organized the work, kept the schedule on track, resolved issues with partners, and built repeatable steps so results were reliable and delivered on time.

Read more
  • National scope: Supported a large network of sites across the country — states, major cities, and territories — representing the great majority of U.S. births. Coordinated intake, reviews, and releases so each site moved smoothly from start to finish.
  • Season planning and coordination: Set the calendar, intake checklist, and checkpoints. Ran standing touchpoints, unblocked issues quickly, and kept everyone aligned on what was needed and when.
  • Data intake and validation: Converted plain-text “birth” and “frame” files into structured datasets using a standard template. Verified counts and identifiers, checked required fields, and documented any quirks before analysis so there were no surprises later.
  • Sampling verification: Reviewed how each site drew its sample, compared expected and observed patterns, and flagged anything unusual for follow-up. Kept a clear record of findings and fixes so the next run started cleaner.
  • Fair results when some people do not respond: Compared who answered with everyone who was eligible, grouped people with similar chances of responding, and adjusted their influence so results reflected the whole population, not just those who replied.
  • Coverage checks: Compared important totals to official counts and looked for signs that certain groups might be missing. Built in stop-points so a run would pause if something needed attention.
  • One-click packaging: Automated the creation of consistent tables, figures, and notes for reviewers. Produced tidy, share-ready documents and a short summary of what was checked and what changed.
  • Lookup-driven, repeatable runs: Used a single “lookup” table to hold the site-specific settings. The system read that row and applied the right rules automatically, which reduced manual edits and made results consistent across sites and seasons.
  • Partner support and problem solving: When a file did not match the plan, worked directly with the site to identify the cause, agree on a correction, and record the exact steps taken so the fix did not have to be rediscovered.
  • Training and handoff: Led short, practical sessions on the process; shared simple checklists and examples; and kept a clear trail of decisions so teammates could reproduce the full run.
  • Outcomes: On-time releases across many jurisdictions, fewer back-and-forth cycles, faster reviews, and clearer paperwork for later audits — with a season playbook that the team can reuse and improve.

PRAMS Nonresponse Policy — Independent Statistical Review and OMB Approval

Jan 2024 – Apr 2025

I served as the independent mathematical statistician who reviewed our team’s analyses on whether a fixed survey response-rate threshold was still needed. I reviewed the models, evaluated the evidence, and wrote the official recommendation to retire the threshold.

Read more
  • Objective review: Assessed multi-year findings, checked that interpretations matched the data, and translated implications into plain language for leadership and partners.
  • Risk–benefit framing: Clarified where bias mattered, when results were still decision-useful, and which safeguards should remain (routine bias checks and clear caveats for small or hard-to-reach groups).
  • Policy pathway: Drafted the recommendation memo, briefed leadership, and prepared communications so grantees knew what would change and why.
  • Outcome: The Office of Management and Budget approved removing the fixed response-rate threshold, shifting focus to transparent evaluation of potential bias rather than a single cutoff.

Census-Style Survey Weighting — Automated Reporting Engine (Confidential Client)

June – September 2025 · Lead Architect (Independent Consultant)

I designed and led a three-part system that turned raw survey files into clear, dependable reports in a single, repeatable run. The process checked the incoming files for common issues, applied well-established methods so results fairly represent the full population, and produced consistent, share-ready documents for decision makers. Everything was built so another person could follow the steps, reproduce the results, and understand why each result looks the way it does.

Read more
  • Program 01 — Setup and quality assurance: I standardized the inputs, defined who should be counted, checked for duplicate identification numbers, handled rare edge cases carefully, and created easy-to-read summaries so reviewers could see what the data looked like before any analysis.
  • Program 02 — Weighting for a fair picture: I used established survey methods to correct for who responded and who did not, so the findings reflect the full group we aimed to study. I added automatic checks that compare important totals to official counts and flag anything unusual, and I wrote the rules in a transparent way so another analyst could follow the logic step by step.
  • Program 03 — Reporting: I generated a consistent set of tables and figures with a single action, packaged as polished, share-ready documents. The reports highlighted key results, included a plain-language explanation of how to read them, and provided an appendix of the important checks that were performed.
  • Reliability and safeguards: I created test versions of the data to prove that the system catches issues, and I added guardrails that stop the run when something looks wrong so mistakes do not make it into the final reports.
  • Documentation and handoff: I wrote a short guide, a flowchart of the process, and a step-by-step set of instructions so future team members can reproduce the full run and find what they need quickly.
  • Collaboration and training: I led walkthroughs, gathered feedback, and incorporated improvements. I kept organized meeting notes and aligned file names and folders so anyone on the team could rerun the process and understand the results.
  • Results: The organization gained a dependable way to turn raw survey files into consistent reports in one step, with clearer reviews, fewer errors, and faster turnarounds.
